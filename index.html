Lesson 3

Scatter Plots and Pearson's Correlation

General Mathematics 11 | Fourth Quarter

S.Y. 2025-2026

Learning Objectives

At the end of this lesson, you should be able to:

  • Distinguish between independent and dependent variables.
  • Describe the form, direction, and strength of an association using a scatter plot.
  • Calculate Pearson's correlation coefficient ($r$) using the table method.
  • Interpret the value of $r$ in the context of bivariate data.

Using a Bivariate Scatter Plot

Bivariate data involves two variables. A scatter plot is a graph that shows the relationship between these two variables.

To identify a relationship, we look for a clear pattern in the points.

No clear pattern (No Association)

Clear Pattern (Association exists)

1. Form of an Association

If an association exists, the points tend to follow a pattern. This is called the form.

Linear Form

Points tend to follow a straight line.

Non-linear Form

Points tend to follow a curved line.

2. Direction of Association

For linear forms, we check if the gradient is positive or negative.

Positive

As $x$ increases, $y$ tends to increase (goes up).

Negative

As $x$ increases, $y$ tends to decrease (goes down).

3. Strength of Association

The strength is a measure of how much "scatter" there is.

Strong
Points clearly follow a single stream.

Moderate
Scatter increases, pattern less clear.

Weak
Large amount of scatter.

Independent and Dependent Variables

When analyzing bivariate data, we identify:

  • Independent Variable ($x$): The input. It is not affected by the other variable. Represented on the horizontal axis.
  • Dependent Variable ($y$): The output. It is "dependent" on the input. Represented on the vertical axis.

Example: Studying for a test.

Independent: Hours spent studying.

Dependent: Score on the test.

Pearson's Correlation Coefficient ($r$)

Pearson's $r$ provides a precise number to measure the strength and direction of a linear association. The value ranges from $-1$ to $+1$.

The Formula

To calculate Pearson's $r$, we use the summation method:

$$ r = \frac{n(\sum xy) - (\sum x)(\sum y)}{\sqrt{[n(\sum x^2) - (\sum x)^2][n(\sum y^2) - (\sum y)^2]} } $$

Where:

  • $n$ = number of pairs of scores
  • $\sum xy$ = sum of the products of paired scores
  • $\sum x$ = sum of $x$ scores
  • $\sum y$ = sum of $y$ scores
  • $\sum x^2$ = sum of squared $x$ scores
  • $\sum y^2$ = sum of squared $y$ scores
